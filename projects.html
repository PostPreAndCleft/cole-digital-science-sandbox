Research program

My program aims to map the structural principles of synaptic organization and turn them into leverage points for intervention, using light and electron microscopy.

Overview

I will identify the proteins that constitute and regulate transsynaptic assemblies, classify common assembly types and link them to synaptic function, and determine how assemblies remodel during activity to sustain plasticity and memory.

Manual tomographic image analysis is a major bottleneck. To unlock tomography at scale, I am developing open, automated analysis tools so the field can detect disease-relevant organizational phenotypes—tether density, PSD fragmentation, assembly disruption—that may indicate early markers of synaptic vulnerabilities in Alzheimer's disease and related disorders.

Project 1: Transsynaptic assemblies in synaptic health and degeneration

The prevalence of transsynaptic assemblies in excitatory and inhibitory synapses suggests they play a fundamental role in synaptic function. Cell adhesion molecules such as neurexins and neuroligins individually influence presynaptic or postsynaptic functions, and also bind together to form cleft-spanning complexes. Understanding the composition and function of core assemblies will reveal how assemblies affect synaptic processes.

Aim 1: Postsynaptic scaffolding knockouts

Investigate structural changes in the presynaptic compartment resulting from knockout of postsynaptic scaffolding proteins (PSD-95 family) in dissociated rat hippocampal neuronal cultures. Immuno-EM will visualize and quantify presynaptic structural changes.

Aim 2: Assembly dynamics during stimulation

Evaluate structural transformations in transsynaptic assemblies using electron tomography on AMPA or high K+ stimulated synaptosomes derived from rat hippocampal tissue. Characterize the number and type of transsynaptic structures to understand how assemblies dissociate with activity.

Aim 3: Mapping assemblies in disease-risk backgrounds

Use APEX2 peroxidase labeling to identify critical transsynaptic assembly structures in electron tomograms of resting and stimulated cultures and isogenic human iPSC-derived neurons with APOE3 or APOE4 alleles. Compare assembly organization and remodeling during activity between healthy and AD-risk backgrounds.

Project 2: Accelerating tomographic analysis with advanced segmentation software

Volumetric microscopy generates valuable, information-rich datasets requiring sophisticated processing methods. Data languishing on drives holds untapped discoveries because time-consuming manual segmentation restricts complete analysis. I am continuing an open-source project with collaborative developers from clinical and basic research backgrounds.

Aim 1: Extend the WebKnossos platform

In collaboration with the Kristen Harris Lab's pyReconstruct team, build on the existing web-based WebKnossos segmentation platform. Extend functionality to support more tomographic modalities including nanoscale electron tomography, correlative light and electron microscopy (CLEM), and clinical imaging such as MRI. Refine the user interface to be accessible to non-specialists.

Aim 2: Plugin system for algorithm contribution

Develop a plug-in system that enables laboratories to contribute advanced algorithms directly to the segmentation platform. This will ease burdens on algorithm developers while lowering barriers to adoption. A robust, ImageJ-like environment for volumetric algorithms will accelerate analysis of volumetric datasets and encourage community-wide adoption across neuroscience.
