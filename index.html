Mapping synaptic architecture at the nanoscale

I study how nanoscale structures at synapses enable the brain to form memories that persist for decades. Using electron tomography, I reveal the hidden organizational strategies—tethers, scaffolds, and assemblies—that allow synapses to achieve the balance of flexibility and stability required for learning and memory.

My work identifies the structural "handles" biology uses to control nanoscale processes, and examines how their disruption may contribute to Alzheimer's disease and related disorders. Across microscopy modalities, datasets now outpace interpretation. I work on the workflow gap—turning complex data into reliable, interpretable evidence. I focus on long-horizon discovery: the multi-step reasoning and validation needed to move from raw data to biological insight.

Research focus

Transsynaptic assemblies

Mapping synapse-spanning structures that link presynaptic machinery to postsynaptic receptor domains, using electron tomography and molecular labeling to understand how assemblies coordinate synaptic function.

Open analysis tools

Developing scalable, open-source software for tomographic analysis. Collaborating with the Harris Lab to extend WebKnossos for nanoscale structure discovery across imaging modalities. Methods include electron tomography and volumetric analysis pipelines for large imaging datasets.

Background

Ph.D. in Neuroscience from Northwestern University. Postdoctoral training at NINDS in the Structural Cell Biology Section under Thomas S. Reese. I have led seminar series at the Marine Biological Laboratory and NINDS, and managed a 7-person team developing advanced segmentation software.
